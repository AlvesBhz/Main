Vale Base Metals

Vale Base MetalsOur OperationsSudbury Complex

Located approximately 400 km north of Toronto, the Greater Sudbury area is renowned for its natural landscapes and rich mining legacy.

A Mining Legacy

Our Sudbury operations have been active for over 140 years, with mining commencing at the Totten Mine back in 1883.

Over time, Sudbury has evolved into one of the  largest mining hubs in the world, playing a pivotal role in the global base metals industry.

About the Operation

Today, our Sudbury operations encompass a robust and integrated operation allowing us to deliver the right mix of low-carbon, high purity and specialized products for our customers with the operational flexibility they require while also optimizing our end-to-end margin.

We produce a diverse portfolio of metals at our Sudbury operations including copper, nickel, cobalt, platinum group metals, gold, and silver. In Sudbury, we have one open-pit mine and operate five underground mines in addition to a mill, smelter, and refinery.

Mining Operations

Totten

  • Underground Mine
  • Started mining in 1883, re-opened in 2014 after being on care and maintenance since the mid-1970s.
  • Deepest current mining level: 4,650 feet

Creighton

Copper Cliff Mine (South and North)

Coleman

  • Resumed operations as an open mine in 2024 in partnership with Thiess and two local Indigenous businesses: Z’Gamok Construction LP, and Aki-eh Dibinwewziwin Limited Partnership

Surface Plants

Our vertically integrated Sudbury operations include milling, refining, and smelting facilities:

Clarabelle Mill

Ore from local mines is transported to Clarabelle Mill, the first stage in mine processing. Commissioned in 1971, ore size is reduced by a SAG (Semi-Autogenous Grinding) / ball mill circuit at Clarabelle to liberate valuable minerals. The minerals are converted into a copper concentrate and a nickel concentrate using a flotation process. The copper concentrate is sold to market while the nickel concentrate is delivered to the smelter complex for further processing.

Copper Cliff Smelter Complex

Commissioned in 1930, the Copper Cliff Smelter Complex uses a pyrometallurgical process to remove sulphur and iron from nickel concentrate to create a nickel matte. The sulphur dioxide is converted to sulphuric acid at an acid plant located in the complex and then sold to market. The nickel matte is delivered to the Copper Cliff Nickel Refinery for final processing.

Copper Cliff Nickel Refinery

Commissioned in 1973, nickel matte undergoes additional processing at the refinery to remove final impurities to make high-purity nickel pellets and powders that are delivered to market to support various industries that require high-nickel alloys such as the aerospace, defense, electronics, nuclear sectors.

Workforce

Our Sudbury operations are supported by a team of over 4,000 direct employees and more than 1,000 contractors, making it one of the largest and most dynamic mining operations in the region.

Environmental Stewardship

Environmental sustainability is a core commitment at Vale Base Metals. Our operations are internationally recognized for their leadership in emissions reductions and land reclamation.

We actively manage and monitor environmental impacts through:

Air Quality Monitoring: Regular reporting on ambient concentrations of total suspended particulates, nickel, cobalt, sulfur dioxide, and other metals.

Environmental Monitoring Team: Comprised of Vale Base Metals representatives, community members, and officials from the Ontario Ministry of the Environment, Conservation and Parks. This team meets annually to review progress and ensure compliance with site-specific air standards and environmental approvals.

Community Engagement

Vale Base Metals is an integral part of the fabric of the Greater Sudbury community. Many of our employees were born and raised there and are the second or third generation of their families to have worked at our operations. Our employees are known for their generosity commitment to giving back. In fact, the country’s most successful annual food drive, the Edgar Burton Christmas Food Drive, was created by a former employee for whom the campaign is named. United Steelworkers Local 6500 and Vale Base Metals support the food drive each year and are also proud of their 48 years of combined support for the United Way Centraide Northeastern Ontario.

Investing back into the community comes in many forms for the Sudbury Operations, including the donation of time and expertise supporting boards and committees to providing equipment that enables hands-on learning opportunities to our local educational partners as well as financial contributions for initiatives that enhance the community.

Dismantling of the Superstack

Watch the video on the engineering process to learn more about the Superstack dismantling

As part of our commitment to environmental stewardship, Vale Base Metals is moving forward with the decommissioning of the iconic Superstack at the Copper Cliff Smelter Complex in Sudbury.

The structure, which went operational in 1972, was decommissioned in 2020 following the successful completion of the approximately $1 billion Clean Atmospheric Emissions Reduction Project, a cornerstone initiative in our ongoing, long-term environmental stewardship program for Sudbury.

The dismantling of the Superstack began in September 2025 and will take about five years to complete. Specialized equipment has been deployed to ensure the procedure fully adheres to the established plan.

Detailed impact mitigation plans have been developed as part of the dismantling process to safeguard our operations and the local community. Dust and noise levels are being actively controlled. Monitoring devices have been installed throughout the area to provide reference data and evaluate the effectiveness of each mitigation measure. Advanced engineering techniques are being implemented to ensure the dismantling is carried out safely and efficiently, supported by a community impact mitigation plan.

FAQs

What has occurred since the superstack was decommissioned in 2020?

Since 2020, we have been preparing the superstack for dismantling. We have spent time slowly cleaning the stack’s interior, removing the steel liner, and sealing or removing accessory structures. We also took time to preform careful study and design work to ensure that when it is eventually dismantled that it is safe for the community and our employees working in the smelter complex.

Why can’t the superstack be re-purposed for another use?

Since the superstack’s decommissioning in 2020, Vale has been approached by various groups with alternatives to dismantling. These ideas are unfeasible based on several factors:

Safety and Access: The superstack is wholly contained within Vale’s Copper Cliff Smelter Complex property and there is no safe viable public access. As an active and busy industrial site, access is strictly controlled and monitored for safety as there are hazardous materials and moving equipment moving throughout the complex. Our policy requires visitors to wear personal protective equipment and be escorted when moving within the smelter.

Practicality: Vale understands and appreciates the superstack’s significance to Sudbury’s identity and history. However, maintaining such a large structure isn’t just about preserving a landmark—it’s about ensuring the safety, viability, and sustainability of that decision. The costs involved aren’t merely financial; they include ongoing safety considerations, environmental impacts, and the infeasibility of adapting an aging industrial structure for modern use. For these reasons, a decision to apply resources to dismantle the superstack is the responsible way to serve current and future generations.

Neighbourhood impact: The superstack and the Copper Cliff Smelter Complex are located directly beside one of Greater Sudbury’s oldest and most historical residential neighbourhoods known as Little Italy. The roads in this neighbourhood are narrow, parking is limited, and land is unavailable. Any proposal that seeks to redevelop the superstack for non-industrial use would increase vehicle traffic and parking needs. This would directly impact the quality of life for residents living in this community.

How will the copperstack concrete shell be taken down?

The concrete exterior will be cut in 4-foot sections. These pieces will be dropped down the middle of the chimney and collected for removal. Watch this animation to see how the process works.

How will you ensure the ongoing dismantling is managed smoothly and safely for the neighbouring community?

Dust will be managed through stringent dust control methods, which include both active and passive strategies. Active methods include water misters, dust suppressants, and dust filtering. Passive methods will include enclosing the work area to capture the dust.

Sound will mitigated by enclosing the work area at the platform level and by using blasting mats at the base of the stack for falling material.

General concerns or questions about this work can be reported to Vale’s Community Concerns Line at (705) 222-8253. Calls are answered 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Alternatively, individuals can email [email protected].

Who will perform the work to dismantle the two chimneys?

QM Environmental will dismantle the copperstack. They are one of Canada’s largest and most trusted demolition contractors. They have extensive experience with complex projects.

ICC Commonwealth is responsible for the superstack dismantling. They specialize in large-scale and piecemeal chimney demolition and have expertise working around the world dismantling large concrete chimney stacks.

How long will it take for each concrete shell to be dismantled?

The copperstack dismantling project is expected to last until the spring of 2025.

The superstack dismantling project will take approximately 5 years to complete. The timeline of this project is longer due to the size of the chimney.

Where will the concrete material go?

The concrete shell will be removed from the Smelter area and placed in a designated area within Vale’s Central Tailings Facility. This location is designed for this purpose and will ensure that the rubble is safely contained, monitored and that all contact water is treated.

Where I can share images, ask a question or share a memory of the superstack!

If you have a question, want to leave a comment, or simply share a memory of the superstack (pictures are welcome), you can email [email protected].

When will the superstack come down?

Since 2020 the superstack has been inactive but work on preparing it for dismantling has not stopped. Careful study and design work was needed to ensure the dismantling was safe for the community and our employees working in the smelter complex. Time was spent slowly cleaning the stack’s interior, removing the steel liner and sealing or removing accessory structures in preparation for a future dismantling of the shell.

We are currently evaluating the approach to its dismantling, and we will keep the community informed as our plans develop.
